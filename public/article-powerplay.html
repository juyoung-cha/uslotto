Is Power Play Worth It? Analyzing the Multiplier Strategy

Category: Strategy Guide | Published: June 22, 2026

When purchasing a Powerball ticket, you are offered an optional feature called "Power Play" for an additional $1 per game. This feature multiplies non-jackpot prizes by 2, 3, 4, 5, or 10 times. To make an informed choice, let's analyze the mathematical structure and expected value of the Power Play option.

1. How Power Play Works

Before the Powerball drawing, a single Power Play multiplier is drawn from a pool of 43 (or 42, depending on the jackpot size) multipliers:

  • 2x Multiplier: 24 balls in the pool.
  • 3x Multiplier: 13 balls in the pool.
  • 4x Multiplier: 3 balls in the pool.
  • 5x Multiplier: 2 balls in the pool.
  • 10x Multiplier: 1 ball in the pool (only active when the advertised jackpot is $150 Million or less).

If you purchase a Power Play ticket and win a non-jackpot prize, your prize is multiplied by the drawn multiplier. The only exception is the Match 5 prize (5 white balls, no Powerball), which is capped at **$2 Million**, regardless of the multiplier drawn.

2. The Probability Breakdown

Let's look at the mathematical probability of each multiplier being drawn when the 10x multiplier is active (jackpot is $150M or less):

  • 2x: 24 / 43 = 55.8% chance
  • 3x: 13 / 43 = 30.2% chance
  • 4x: 3 / 43 = 7.0% chance
  • 5x: 2 / 43 = 4.7% chance
  • 10x: 1 / 43 = 2.3% chance

If the jackpot is over $150 Million, the 10x ball is removed, changing the pool size to 42. In both scenarios, you have a roughly **86% chance** of drawing a 2x or 3x multiplier, meaning the higher multipliers are relatively rare.

3. Strategic Value: Is It Worth It?

To determine if the extra $1 is worth the cost, look at how the multiplier affects the different prize tiers:

  • The Match 5 Tier: If you match 5 white balls, the standard prize is $1,000,000. With Power Play, it automatically doubles to **$2,000,000**, regardless of the multiplier drawn. The odds of matching 5 white balls are 1 in 11,688,053.
  • The Match 4 + Powerball Tier: The standard prize is $50,000. With Power Play, a 2x multiplier pays $100,000, and a 10x multiplier pays $500,000. The odds of matching this tier are 1 in 913,129.
  • Lower Tiers: For smaller prizes, Power Play can be very satisfying. For example, matching 3 white balls ($7) becomes $14 to $70, and matching just the Powerball ($4) becomes $8 to $40.

Mathematically, adding Power Play slightly increases the **expected value** of a ticket, but it does not change your odds of winning a prize. It simply changes the payout structure. If you are playing specifically to win the jackpot, Power Play has no value because it does not multiply the grand prize.

Conclusion: When to Play

If your goal is to win the jackpot or nothing, skip the Power Play and save your money. However, if you want to maximize your returns on secondary prizes, Power Play is a good option. It is especially attractive when the jackpot is under $150 Million and the 10x multiplier is active, or if you want to secure a $2 Million payout instead of $1 Million for matching the five white balls.
