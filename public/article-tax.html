Lottery Tax Guide: Federal & State Tax Implications on Jackpots

Category: Financial Planning | Published: June 22, 2026

If you win a major Powerball or Mega Millions jackpot, the gross jackpot amount advertised is not what you will take home. In the United States, lottery winnings are treated as ordinary taxable income. This means your windfall is subject to both federal and state income taxes. Let's walk through exactly how much the government takes and how much you will keep.

1. Federal Taxes: The Initial Withholding vs. Actual Tax Owed

The moment you claim a high-tier prize, the IRS immediately collects tax via a mandatory federal withholding. This is a flat rate deducted automatically before the money is distributed to you:

  • Standard Federal Withholding: 24% for U.S. citizens and residents with a valid Social Security Number.
  • Non-Resident Withholding: 30% for international players who win a U.S. jackpot.

However, the 24% withholding is just a prepayment. Because a jackpot places you squarely into the highest federal tax bracket (which is currently **37%** for income over $600,000), you will owe an additional **13%** of federal income tax when you file your returns the following year. Winning hundreds of millions of dollars guarantees you will pay the maximum marginal rate on almost the entire sum.

2. State Taxes: Location is Everything

In addition to federal taxes, the state where you purchase the ticket will tax your winnings. State tax rates vary dramatically, ranging from 0% to nearly 11%. Here is a breakdown of state tax brackets for lottery winnings:

State Tax Tier State Tax Rate Applicable States
No Tax States 0.0% California, Florida, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, Wyoming
Low Tax States 3.0% - 5.0% Pennsylvania (3.07%), Indiana (3.23%), Ohio (3.99%), Illinois (4.95%)
Medium Tax States 5.0% - 8.0% Georgia (5.75%), North Carolina (5.25%), New Jersey (8.0%)
High Tax States 8.0% - 10.9% Maryland (8.95%), New York (10.9%)

Note: California and Delaware do not tax in-state lottery winnings. Florida, Texas, and Washington do not have a state income tax at all, meaning they automatically do not tax lottery winnings. However, if you purchase a ticket in New York, you will face the highest state tax rate of 10.9%, plus an additional municipal tax of 3.876% if you are a resident of New York City.

3. An Example Calculation

Imagine you win a cash-value jackpot of **$100 Million** in a state with no income tax (e.g., Florida):

  1. Gross Amount: $100,000,000
  2. Federal Withholding (24%): -$24,000,000
  3. Initial Payout: $76,000,000
  4. Additional Federal Tax Owed (13%): -$13,000,000 (settled when filing taxes)
  5. Total Take-Home: **$63,000,000**

In this scenario, you keep 63% of the jackpot. If you won that same prize in New York City, your combined federal, state, and city tax would exceed 50%, leaving you with around **$48,000,000**.

Conclusion: Tax-Efficiency is Critical

Because taxes consume such a large portion of your winnings, consulting with a certified public accountant (CPA) and a lottery lawyer immediately after winning is crucial. They can help you structure your payout, set up trusts, plan charitable deductions, and offset taxes using write-offs, protecting as much of your newfound wealth as possible.
